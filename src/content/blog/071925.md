If I could go back in time and start my journey from scratch, I would do a few things differently. Not because I did anything wrong, but because I’ve learned that there are more conscious and efficient ways to become a complete software engineer, even without prior experience.

Instead of focusing only on trendy languages or current frameworks, I would concentrate on building a foundation that would allow me to adapt to any future technology. This approach combines technical fundamentals, consistent practice, and a strategic mindset about my career.

## Start with the Fundamentals, Not the Tools

The biggest temptation for a beginner is to jump straight to the "finish line"—learning React, Java Spring Boot, or Go, and trying to build something complex. I would do the opposite. I would focus on understanding what happens under the hood.

### Programming Fundamentals with C and C++

These languages aren't just about syntax. They force you to understand low-level concepts like memory management and pointers. Why is this important? Because by understanding how memory is allocated and freed, you learn to write more efficient code and diagnose problems more deeply, which is a rare and valuable skill.

### Data Structures and Algorithms (DSA) with Python

Many newcomers fear DSA, but it's the skeleton of engineering thought. I would use Python not to get lost in syntax, but to focus on the logic. 

By solving algorithm problems, you're not just training for interviews; you're learning to analyze the complexity of a solution and choose the best tool for each problem. It's like learning to use a scalpel before performing surgery.

> A solid foundation in computer science is what differentiates a "coder" from an "engineer."  
> The coder knows how to use the tool; the engineer understands how it works and when to use it.

## The Alternative to Formal Education: Learn by Doing

Not everyone has the opportunity or the resources for a formal degree, and that's okay. There are equally valid and high-quality paths that only require discipline and proactivity.

### Strategic Online Courses

Instead of jumping from one course to another, I would choose one that gives me a comprehensive view and teaches me how to think.

**Recommendation:** [Harvard's CS50](https://cs50.harvard.edu) — it doesn’t teach you a single language; it teaches you to think like a computer scientist, using practical examples.

### Personal Projects Focused on Solutions

The biggest mistake is building "portfolio projects" that don't solve anything. What companies really want to see is your ability to solve problems.

I would create a project that solves a real problem for me or someone I know. This doesn't have to be complex. It could be:

* A small automation script to organize files
* A script that monitors product prices online
* A simple game

The value is in the process: planning, building, failing, debugging, and refactoring.

> Learning by doing is what turns theoretical knowledge into practical experience.  
> It's like the difference between reading a book about swimming and actually jumping into the pool.

## Build a Portfolio That Tells a Story

Your portfolio shouldn't just be a list of projects on GitHub; it should be a narrative about your growth.

### GitHub Repositories with Documentation

For each project, I would write a good `README.md` that explains:

- The problem I was solving
- The architectural decisions I made (even if simple)
- The technologies used
- The challenges I faced

This shows the maturity of your thinking and your communication skills.

### Contribute to Open-Source Projects

Instead of building everything from scratch, I would look for open-source projects to contribute to. This:

- Exposes you to real-world codebases
- Teaches you to work in a team
- Helps you follow quality standards

Even small contributions like fixing bugs or improving documentation are valuable.

> A well-structured portfolio with projects that solve problems shows that you not only know how to code,  
> but also how to think like an engineer.

## The Art of Entering the Market

Many people wait until they feel 100% ready before applying for a job, but this mindset is a mistake. The selection process itself is a form of learning.

### Seek Learning Opportunities, Not Just Jobs

Instead of focusing only on salaries, I would apply for:

- Internships
- Trainee programs

These positions are designed for your development. The experience and mentorship you gain outweigh the initial salary.

### Prioritize Autonomy

In the age of AI, the most important skill is the ability to actively seek knowledge. I would challenge myself to solve problems on my own before asking for help from ChatGPT or Stack Overflow.

This doesn’t mean isolation — it means learning to debug and research independently.

## Final Thoughts

Every project, every interview, and every mistake is a chance to learn. A growth mindset is key.

> Understand the trade-offs of every decision you make.  
> Why did I use a list instead of a dictionary?  
> Why did I choose this API architecture?

Thinking about these choices early on is what builds an engineering mindset.

> If you're starting now, remember:  
> The path to becoming a software engineer is like cultivating a tree.  
> You don't make it grow overnight, but you nurture it daily.  
> Patience, persistence, and attention to detail are essential.  
> And instead of seeking the fastest fruit, you'll be building the roots that will sustain you throughout your life.
